title = "on digital impermanence"
date = 2026-03-20
description = "everything you build will be mass-collected eventually"
[taxonomies]
tags = ["philosophy", "systems"]
+++

i used to think digital meant permanent. that putting something on a server was like carving it into stone. i was wrong about this in the way that only someone who grew up on the internet could be wrong -- completely, confidently, and with a backup that also no longer exists. i have lost bookmarks to dead domains, music to shuttered services, conversations to platforms that pivoted into something unrecognizable. the internet is not an archive. it is a river, and most of what touches the water is carried away.

there was a small forum i used to post on in the early 2010s. maybe two hundred active users. people shared code snippets, talked about window managers, argued about text editors with the kind of passion that only matters when you are seventeen. the domain expired in 2019 and nobody renewed it. the wayback machine caught fragments -- a thread here, a profile page there -- but most of it is just gone. all those conversations, all that accumulated knowledge, dissolved like they were never there. i think about those people sometimes. some of them probably still use the same handle on other platforms. we would not recognize each other.

there is a japanese concept -- mono no aware -- the bittersweet awareness of impermanence. the cherry blossoms are beautiful precisely because they fall. i wonder if the same applies to the things we build with code. maybe a project does not need to last forever to have mattered. maybe the deploy that ran for six months and helped three people was enough. we are so obsessed with scale and permanence that we forget most valuable things in life are temporary. a good conversation. a well-structured function. a server that stayed up exactly as long as it needed to.

```
$ uptime
 14:23:07 up 847 days, 3:42, 1 user, load average: 0.12, 0.08, 0.03
```

847 days. that is impressive until the power goes out, or the drive fails, or you forget to renew the domain, or the company behind the cloud provider decides your tier is not profitable anymore. every system is temporary. every uptime counter is just a countdown in disguise. i do not say this to be nihilistic. i say it because acceptance is the first step toward building things that matter for the time they exist, rather than pretending they will exist forever.

you are going to carry that weight -- the weight of things you built that no longer run, of communities that scattered, of code that compiled for the last time without anyone knowing it was the last time. the trick is not to build things that last. the trick is to build things worth remembering, and to let them go when the time comes. `rm -rf` with grace.
